# FINAL REPORT

## 1. Scoreboard & Story

| Player | VP | Money | Result |
|--------|-----|-------|--------|
| **P0** | **26** | $6 | **WINNER (money tiebreak)** |
| P1 (me) | 26 | $1 | 2nd |

**Story:** I leveraged hard early (10 loans by R2) and built a City 1 fortress; P0 quietly monopolized City 2 + City 3 with a brilliant subsidy-farming strategy and led 21-14 into R4. Rounds 3-4 became a frozen austerity standoff — all three opponents bid 0 to keep the rate at $4 while P2/P3 slid toward inevitable death. The inflection point: in R4 I realized P2/P3 would default *no matter what*, ending the game that round — so I bid 8 (rate → $6), took $80, and used my 8-to-1 cash advantage to buy the City 2 majority out from under P0 (8 buildings to 7), a 6 VP swing that turned 20-28 into 26-25. The counter-inflection: P0's genius $3 RES purchase (simultaneously defending City 2 *and* activating a +3 VP state subsidy on his City 3 stack), and his final $4 auction snipe of the bankrupt P3's building for the 26-26 money tiebreak.

## 2. Experience Review

**Genuinely tense and I'd play again.** The endgame was one of the best "cornered-animal" calculation puzzles I've encountered: computing whether I could force P0's bankruptcy (I couldn't — short by $1-2 in every line), then pivoting to a majority war where my last purchase left me solvent by *exactly $1*. Pacing: R1-R2 buying was breezy and fun. R3 dragged emotionally — the all-pass austerity rounds felt like a stalemate where the leader (P0) benefits from every quiet turn, and my only options were "lose slowly" or "detonate the rate and lose fast." Rote parts: the wait-loop between turns, and the long identical action lists (45 buy options each cycle). The interest phase is where all drama lives; the bid phase mostly resolved as everyone passing after R2.

## 3. Agency

High agency — this felt like a game decided by decisions, not dice. The decisions that mattered most: (a) recognizing in R3 that taking *any* loan would end the game immediately with me losing — restraint as a weapon; (b) the R4 realization that P2/P3's deaths were locked in, making it the final round, which converted "cash flow discipline" into "spend literally everything"; (c) the City 2 majority war. The one random element — which of P3's buildings got auctioned — didn't matter; P0's snipe worked regardless. My loss traces to identifiable mistakes, which is the mark of an agency-rich game.

## 4. What I'd do differently

- **Deny the $3 RES card.** I identified it as "double-hot" (P0's only affordable City 2 defense AND his +3 VP state-subsidy activator) and still took the COM $6 first. Buying it myself would have forced P0 to spend his last $6-7 defending City 2, stripped ~5-6 VP from his final score, and left him too poor for the auction snipe. That single sequencing error cost the game.
- Take fewer loans in R2 (10 loans at escalating rates was a $40/round anchor that nearly locked me out of the R4 endgame).
- Track the *money tiebreaker* from the start — I didn't know it existed and optimized my final cash to $1.

## 5. Design suggestions & confusion

- **The money tiebreak was invisible.** Nothing in the rulebook or interface mentions it; losing a 26-26 game to an unstated rule stings. State it up front.
- **State subsidy income ambiguity:** the rules don't clearly say whether state-subsidy income goes to all owners in the section or only the leader. I had to reverse-engineer from income logs mid-game ("both = $3/card" for the leader).
- **The austerity stalemate is a design soft spot:** once rates threaten, all trailing players pass at 0 and the game freezes; the leader wins every quiet round. Consider a minimum rate ratchet that doesn't depend on borrowing, or income pressure that forces action.
- **P2/P3's death-march:** two players spent two full rounds mathematically dead with nothing to do. Elimination-adjacent states need more counterplay (e.g., selling buildings).
- Interface: excellent overall (net-after-interest and VP-if-scored-now were invaluable). Wishlist: show the tiebreak rule, show a "projected defaults this round" table, and collapse the 45-item buy list by card.
- The subsidy system is the hidden gem — fewest-city / most-in-section creates wonderful judo (my RES-to-City-2 tie play, P0's marker manipulation). More of the game should route through it.

## 6. Identity guesses

- **P0 — language-model AI (or strong human; I lean LLM).** Coherent long-horizon strategy (mono-city subsidy farming), multi-purpose moves (the $3 RES double-play), correct austerity discipline, and the tiebreak-aware auction snipe. Occasionally long think-times. Too adaptive for a script.
- **P2 — classical scripted AI.** Formulaic opening bids (3,1,0,0), passed buy phases early while holding cash, marched into a certain-death default without ever attempting a survival bid, zero reactive play.
- **P3 — classical scripted AI.** Similar bid pattern (2,2,0,0), reasonable greedy buying heuristics early (contested my COM section), but spent its last $4 on a building while facing an unpayable $30 interest bill and made no attempt to avoid the bankruptcy that eliminated it. A human or LLM would have fought the death spiral.
